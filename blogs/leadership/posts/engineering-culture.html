What Engineering Culture Actually Means

Culture isn't a ping-pong table or a Slack emoji. It is the accumulated residue of every decision a team makes under pressure — and it can be shaped deliberately.

Shreedhar Kodate · 03 March 2024 · 8 min read
culture leadership teams
Contents

    What Culture Is Not

    Walk into the office of any startup that values culture and you'll find the same inventory: standing desks, exposed brick, a phrase on the wall about moving fast, and a kitchen full of snacks. These are props. They are not culture.

    Culture is not a benefit. It is not a policy. It is not a set of stated values that nobody refers to after the all-hands where they were announced.

    Culture is what happens when the manager is not in the room.

    The Residue of Decisions

    The most useful definition I've encountered comes from Peter Drucker's framing — paraphrased endlessly — that culture eats strategy for breakfast. But a more operational version is this:

    Culture is the accumulated residue of decisions made under pressure.

    When a deadline arrives and you have to choose between shipping a known bug and delaying — what you choose, consistently, over time, is your culture. When a junior engineer disagrees with a principal in a review — whether they feel safe saying so is your culture. When a postmortem happens after an outage — whether the narrative looks for systems failures or individual blame is your culture.

    Observation Audit your last five tough decisions as a team. The pattern you find is a more honest statement of culture than any value you've written down.

    Three Levers That Actually Work

    1. What You Celebrate

    What gets recognised in public — in Slack, in retros, in performance reviews — signals what the team values. If you celebrate shipping velocity, people will cut corners on quality. If you celebrate learning in public (including failures), people will share more and hide less.

    The lever is narrow but powerful: change what you applaud, and within two quarters the ambient behaviour shifts.

    2. What You Tolerate

    Leaders define the floor of acceptable behaviour, not the ceiling. Every time a condescending code review comment goes unaddressed, every time a missed deadline is normalised, you've moved the floor down.

    The corollary is equally true: you cannot define culture by what you aspire to. You define it by what you are willing to call out.

    3. Who You Hire and Promote

    Hiring in one's own image is the most common way cultures calcify. Deliberately hiring people who challenge the existing mental models — and then promoting those who demonstrate the values you want to grow — is the longest-horizon but highest-leverage tool available.

    A Note Specific to ML Teams

    Machine learning teams face a particular culture challenge: the work is uncertain. Most experiments fail. The team that builds a norm of honest uncertainty — "we don't know yet, here is what we're testing" — outperforms the team that builds a norm of bravado.

    Psychological safety isn't a soft goal. In ML teams it is directly upstream of research quality: when people are afraid to report null results, the team compounds on sunk costs instead of pivoting.

    Reference Google's Project Aristotle found that psychological safety was the single strongest predictor of team effectiveness — above individual talent, structure, or incentives. [1]

    A Practical Starting Point

    If you are in a position to influence culture and don't know where to begin, start with one small ritual: a weekly five-minute slot in your team meeting where someone shares something they tried that didn't work and what they learned.

    It signals that failure is a data point. Over time it creates permission to be honest. And permission to be honest is the foundation on which everything else worth building can be built.
