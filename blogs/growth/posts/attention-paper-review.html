Paper Review: Attention Is All You Need

Vaswani et al., NeurIPS 2017. A structured review covering the core contribution, what I understood quickly, what took longer, and what to read next.

Shreedhar Kodate · January 2024 · 7 min review · Done
paper-review transformers nlp
Contents
    Paper at a Glance Title: Attention Is All You Need
    Authors: Vaswani, Shazeer, Parmar, Uszkoreit et al.
    Venue: NeurIPS 2017
    One-line: Replaces RNNs/CNNs in seq2seq with pure self-attention, achieving SOTA on WMT translation at lower training cost.
    My rating: ★★★★★ — essential reading

    Core Contribution

    The Transformer is the first sequence transduction model built entirely on attention mechanisms, without any recurrence or convolution. This removes the sequential dependency that prevented RNNs from being parallelised, and establishes a new architecture family that now underlies almost all large language models.

    The key claim: for sequence modelling tasks, attention can fully replace recurrence, and doing so both improves quality and dramatically reduces training time.

    What I Understood Quickly

    • The motivation against RNNs: sequential computation, long-range dependency path length.
    • The mechanics of scaled dot-product attention — straightforward once you see the Q/K/V framing.
    • Multi-head attention as parallel attention sub-spaces (analogous to using multiple convolutional filter sizes).
    • The encoder-decoder structure and why causal masking is needed in the decoder.

    What Confused Me (and How I Resolved It)

    Why divide by √d_k?

    The paper says large dot products push softmax into regions of small gradients, but it takes a moment to see why this happens at scale. The key insight: for random unit-variance Q and K vectors of dimension d_k, the dot product has variance d_k — so dividing by √d_k restores unit variance regardless of dimension.

    Why sine/cosine for positional encoding?

    The paper's argument about linear interpolation to unseen positions is elegant but compressed. A clearer version: sin(pos + k) can be written as a linear combination of sin(pos) and cos(pos), so the model can learn to attend to "offset by k" positions via linear projection. This also means relative position is representable — which turns out to matter a great deal in later work.

    What I Would Test If I Were the Author

    • Learnable positional encodings were ablated briefly. I'd want a more thorough comparison across longer sequences.
    • The paper evaluates on machine translation only. The architecture's applicability to other sequence tasks (which we now know is vast) is left implicit.
    • Attention head visualisation is included but informal. A systematic interpretability study would have been valuable.

    Connections & Follow-Ons

    Paper Relationship Priority
    BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) Encoder-only Transformer, bidirectional pre-training High
    GPT (Radford et al., 2018) Decoder-only Transformer, autoregressive LM pre-training High
    Relative Attention (Shaw et al., 2018) Improves positional encoding with relative positions Medium
    Sparse Transformer (Child et al., 2019) Addresses O(n²) attention cost for long sequences Medium
    Vision Transformer ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) Applies the same architecture to image patches High

    Implementation Notes

    For a from-scratch PyTorch implementation with detailed annotations, see my companion technical post: Attention Is All You Need — Practitioner's Walkthrough.

    Learning Path Suggested sequence for someone new to transformers:
    1. Read sections 1–3 of the paper first (motivation + architecture).
    2. Work through The Annotated Transformer (Rush, 2018).
    3. Implement multi-head attention from scratch before reading the rest.
    4. Return to sections 4–6 (training, experiments) with working code context.
