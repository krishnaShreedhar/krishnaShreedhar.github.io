The Practice of Gratitude in a Distracted World

Gratitude is not a feeling you wait for — it is a discipline you show up to. Some thoughts on what that looks like in practice.

Shreedhar Kodate · 20 February 2024 · 5 min read
gratitude practice well-being
Contents

    A Misunderstood Word

    Gratitude has been colonised by the self-help industry and reduced to a morning journal exercise. Write three things you're grateful for. Feel better. Move on.

    This is not wrong. But it mistakes the symptom for the disease. Gratitude isn't primarily a technique for improving your mood. It is a reorientation of where you place your attention — and that reorientation takes time, failure, and repetition before it becomes stable.

    Gratitude is not the automatic feeling of thankfulness when good things happen. It is the practised capacity to notice what is already here.

    The Problem With Distraction

    The modern environment is optimised against gratitude. Not maliciously — but structurally. Every feed, notification, and headline is tuned to carry your attention toward what is missing, threatened, or wrong. This is not a conspiracy. It is just the economics of attention: anxiety is more engaging than contentment.

    Gratitude therefore requires an act of counter-intention. It asks you to look in a direction the environment is not pointing you.

    What Works

    Specificity, Not Generality

    "I am grateful for my family" is a thought. "I am grateful that my mother called yesterday and we talked for an hour about nothing in particular" is an experience that can be felt.

    The more specific the object of gratitude, the more the body can participate. Generalities live in the head. Specifics land in the chest.

    The Subtraction Exercise

    This is adapted from the work of psychologist Martin Seligman [1]: instead of adding to a gratitude list, try mentally removing something from your life and sitting with what that absence would mean.

    What would this day look like without electricity? Without the friend who sent a message this morning? Without the health that lets you read these words?

    Subtraction reveals value faster than addition because it engages loss aversion — the same cognitive tendency that makes anxiety so sticky — in the service of presence.

    Say It Out Loud

    Gratitude kept private is a nice thought. Gratitude expressed becomes something relational — it connects you to the person, the moment, or the source of the good.

    A simple rule: once a day, say thank you for something specific to someone specific. Not as a performance. As a transfer of attention toward something that deserves it.

    A Note on Difficult Times

    None of this is an argument for toxic positivity. There are seasons when grief, anger, or exhaustion are the appropriate response to what is happening, and attempting to override those feelings with gratitude exercises is counterproductive.

    But even in difficult seasons, there is often a small honest thing that is worth noting — not to escape the difficulty, but to keep it in proportion.

    A practice At the end of the day, find one moment — however small — that was unambiguously good. Name it specifically. Let it exist alongside everything else that was hard. You are not denying the difficulty. You are refusing to let it be the only thing.
